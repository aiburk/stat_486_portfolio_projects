Introduction

The birthwt dataset has data collected by David W. Hosmer & Stanley Lemeshow at Baystate Medical Center, Springfield, Massachusetts during 1986, with 189 observations, and variables including infant birth weight in grams, mothers weight in pounds at last menstrual period, and smoking status during pregnancy. This analysis is for studying how mothers weight at last menstrual period and smoking status affect infant birth weight. (Source: Hosmer, D.W. and Lemeshow, S. (1989) Applied Logistic Regression. New York: Wiley)

Results

Because the response variable (Birth weight) is continuous and the explanatory variables include a continuous & categorical variable, we will fit a multiple linear regression model for birth weight on last menstrual period weight and smoking status. A scatterplot with regression lines indicates no significant interaction between the explanatory variables.

The structure of the multiple regression model is \[Y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1X_{i,1}+\beta_2X_{i,2}+\epsilon_i\] where \(Y_i\) is birth weight, \(X_{i,1}\) is the mothers last menstrual period weight, \(X_{i,2}\) is smoking status (No = 0, Yes = 1), & \(\epsilon_i\) is the error.

The 95% confidence interval for the effect of mothers last menstrual period weight on birth weight is 0.9, 7.57, which means that with 95% confidence, when last menstrual period weight increases by 1 pound, birth weight increases between 0.9 & 7.57 grams.

The 95% confidence interval for the effect of smoking status on birth weight is -480.39, -63.77, which means that with 95% confidence, the change in birth weight between not smoking during pregnancy & smoking during pregnancy is a decrease between 63.77 & 480.39 grams.

Discussion

Using data collected from Baystate Medical Center, Springfield, Massachusetts by David W. Hosmer & Stanley Lemeshow in 1986, we performed multiple linear regression analysis, modelling infant birth weight on mothers weight at last menstrual period and smoking status during pregnancy. We found that increasing weight at last menstrual period is associated with a slight increase in infant birth weight, and when comparing smoking status, smoking during pregnancy is associated with a generally lower birth weight than not smoking during pregnancy.

We dont have enough information to assess our independence assumption, and the data does not appear to be in temporal order. The normality assumption is reasonably met as indicated by a normal quantile-quantile plot. Our assumption of constant variance is also reasonably met as the residuals show little to no variability.

Because the data were not randomized as far as we know, our results on the effect of last menstrual period weight and smoking status on infant birth weight cannot be interpreted as causal, and since the data are not a random sample from any population, we cannot justifiably extend these results to a larger population.
